A volcano is an opening in a planet's crust. Molten rock rises through it and reaches the surface. The molten rock underground is called magma. Once it erupts, it is called lava. Magma comes from the mantle, the hot layer beneath the crust. The crust is broken into large plates. The plates move slowly over the mantle. Most volcanoes sit at the edges of these plates.

There are three main settings. The first is a subduction zone. Here one plate slides beneath another. The second is a mid-ocean ridge. Here two plates pull apart. The third is a hotspot. Here a plume of hot rock rises in the middle of a plate. Each setting produces a different kind of volcano.

Magma is the central actor. It is less dense than the rock around it. So it rises, the way a bubble rises through water. As it rises, the pressure on it drops. Dissolved gases come out of it. The gases are mostly water vapour and carbon dioxide. They collect and expand. This expansion is what drives an eruption.

The magma's chemistry decides how the eruption goes. The key ingredient is silica. Silica makes magma thick and sticky. Thick magma traps its gas. The trapped gas builds pressure until the magma bursts. That is an explosive eruption. Magma with little silica is thin and runny. Its gas escapes gently. That is a quiet, effusive eruption. So the same rising magma can ooze or explode, depending on what it is made of.

These differences shape the mountain. Thick magma piles up near the vent. It builds a steep cone called a stratovolcano. Thin magma flows far before it cools. It builds a wide, gentle dome called a shield volcano. The mountain, then, is a record of the magma that made it.

The eruption also creates the hazards. Each hazard traces back to the magma and its gas. An explosive eruption shatters magma into ash. The ash mixes with gas and rushes downhill. This is a pyroclastic flow. It moves faster than a person can run. It is the deadliest volcanic hazard. Ash also mixes with rain or snow. That makes a fast mud river called a lahar. A lahar follows the valleys and buries the towns in them. Lava flows are slower. They destroy buildings but rarely kill. Volcanic gas is quieter still. It can pool in a hollow and suffocate anyone there.

Because the hazards trace back to the magma, watching the magma gives warning. Rising magma cracks the rock above it. The cracks release small earthquakes. The earthquakes climb as the magma climbs. Rising magma also swells the ground. Instruments and satellites measure the swelling. And rising magma releases more gas. A change in the gas at the summit signals fresh magma below. None of these signs is a forecast alone. Together they form a pattern. Scientists read the pattern to judge whether an eruption is near.

Even so, the warning is limited. The signs say an eruption is likely. They cannot say the exact day or size. The size depends on how much gas the magma holds. No instrument reads that directly. So a forecast gives a window and a chance, not a date. To fill the gap, scientists study past eruptions. A volcano tends to repeat its own habits. Its old deposits record what it has done before. Reading those deposits sets the baseline for the live signals.

People are the final piece. A volcano is only dangerous where people live near it. And people do live near volcanoes, in large numbers. Volcanic soil is unusually fertile. The gentle flanks are easy to farm and build on. So people settle exactly where the next eruption will reach. The reward and the danger sit in the same place. This is why the work ends in evacuation plans and hazard maps. The eruption cannot be stopped. The people can be moved. Moving them in time is the one thing that saves lives.
