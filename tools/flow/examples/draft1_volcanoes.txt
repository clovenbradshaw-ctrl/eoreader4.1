A volcano forms where magma from the mantle couples to the crust and breaks through it. The coupling is the whole story: a plume of buoyant rock rises, it meets a plate boundary, and the pressure it carries drives the eruption that builds the mountain. Subduction feeds most of them, because a descending slab releases water into the wedge above it, the water lowers the melting point of the rock, and the melt it produces ascends. Hotspots feed the rest, where a fixed plume in the mantle burns through a moving plate and leaves a chain of cones behind it. The relation between the plume and the plate sets the type. A thick continental plate over a wet subduction zone yields a steep stratovolcano, because the magma is viscous and traps its gas. A thin oceanic plate over a hotspot yields a broad shield, because the magma is fluid and flows far.

The magma's chemistry governs the violence. Silica-rich magma is stiff, so it holds its dissolved gas until the pressure drop near the surface forces the gas out all at once, and the eruption is explosive. Silica-poor magma is runny, so the gas escapes gently, and the eruption is effusive. The dissolved water amplifies this, because water comes out of solution first and drives the fragmentation of the magma into ash. The ash couples to the atmosphere and spreads, and the same eruption that buries a valley in pyroclastic flow can cool the whole planet for a year by loading the stratosphere with sulfate.

The hazards follow from these couplings, not from the mountain itself. A pyroclastic flow is a ground-hugging current of gas and particles that races down the flank faster than anyone can outrun it, and it kills by heat and asphyxiation. A lahar is a slurry of ash and water that follows the river valleys and buries the towns built on the old deposits, because those towns sit where the last lahar laid down flat, fertile ground. Lava flows destroy property but rarely kill, because they move slowly enough to walk away from. Gas kills silently, where carbon dioxide pools in a hollow and displaces the air. Tephra collapses roofs under its weight and grounds aircraft by melting inside their engines.

Monitoring reads the couplings before the eruption. Magma moving into the edifice breaks rock, and the breaking registers as swarms of small earthquakes that migrate upward as the magma climbs. The ground itself deforms, tilting and swelling as the reservoir inflates, and satellites and tiltmeters catch the swelling. The gases change, because rising magma degasses, and a shift in the ratio of carbon to sulfur at the summit fumaroles marks fresh magma near the surface. None of these signals is a forecast on its own; the forecast is the pattern they make together, the same way the eruption is the pattern the plume and the plate make together. An observatory that watches all three can raise the alert level in time to move people, and the moving of people is the only thing that has ever reduced the death toll.

Prediction remains coarse because the system is a threshold system. The signals say an eruption is likely within weeks; they cannot say which day, or how large, because the size depends on how much gas the magma is carrying and that is the one variable underground that no instrument reads directly. So the science hedges, and the hedging is honest: it commits to a window and a probability, not a date. The record of past eruptions fills the gap the instruments leave, because a volcano tends to repeat its own style, and the deposits from previous eruptions are a library of what this particular volcano does. A geologist maps those deposits, dates them, and reads the recurrence interval off them, and the interval sets the baseline the live monitoring is measured against.

The people are the last term in every one of these relations. A volcano is only a hazard where people live on it, and people live on volcanoes because volcanic soil is the richest farmland on Earth and the flanks are gentle enough to build on. So the danger concentrates exactly where the reward does, and the same fertility that draws the farmer is the deposit that the next lahar will move. This is why volcanology ends in evacuation planning and land-use maps rather than in the rock: the eruption is a geophysical fact, but the disaster is a human arrangement, and only the human arrangement can be changed in time.
